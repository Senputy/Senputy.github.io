The timeline of coding related events

From 1679 to 2008: humanity's greatest accomplishments with the influence of coding

1679

The concept of coding is originated from a German mathematician whose name was Gottfried Leibniz. He is famed for the recognition of a system which is based on two digits: 0 and 1, also known as binary. His inspiration was to make a mechanical calculator which would calculate automatically based on the amount of marbles that falls through an open hole and the amount that remains at a closed hole. The open and close holes resembled true and false as does 1 and 0 in modern coding. The calculated was never built, but Leibniz’s ideas were of importance and thus making him the first of many individuals who utilized binary to solve problems.

More on Gottfried Leibniz

Gottfried Leibniz was born in 1646 and died in 1716. He was an engineer, a mathematician, a physicist, and a logician. Throughout his duties as an official historian and a legal advisor, he traveled in many countries in Europe and met numerous intellectual mathematicians and physicists whom he had shared ideas with. He worked and engineered a variety of calculating machines, clocks, and even mining machinery. Leibniz was an obvious polymath of broad mathematics, and as a result he had made brilliant discoveries which had impacted the modern world. Some of his most famed groundbreaking discoveries include topology, the integral & differential calculus (Also recognized by Isaac Newton a few years later), and the binary system which he had perfected in 1679.

1804

After more than a century, the concept of binary was used by a French weaver named Joseph Jacquard. His invention: an automated steam-powered weaving loom, consisted of cardboards which were used as the template to poke holes through. The absence or appearance of holes poked through the cardboard ultimately determined a certain pattern that the loom weaves. Although Jacquard’s invention is not much compared to contemporary machines, it made manual looming labor much more relaxing in France. Jacquard also sold many of his machines including the cardboards for extra profit. He died a quiet and comfortable death in 1834.

More on Joseph Jacquard

Jacquard was born in Lyon, France in 1752. His father died in 1772 and left Jacquard his family house and a hand loom which he had ran in a silk factory. Jacquard had served in the French Army when he was 38 and after that he had built his automated steam-powered weaving loom, which he had demonstrated in Paris and won himself a patent and a medal. In 1806 his invention was public and he was given pension and a royal payment for each machine created. His invention was crucial to the advancement of Babbage’s Analytical Engine, which in essence might have affected the overall technological development of the human race.

1842-1843

Jacquard’s idea was later improved by British mathematician Charles Babbage. Babbage thought of a machine that would take in binary information and turn them into instructions. It was named the ‘Analytical Engine’. Although Babbage was uncertain of how to design the machine, fellow Mathematician Ada Lovelace who was also fluent in the study of binary was able to thoroughly explain how Babbage’s machinery could have worked. However, the machine was never built.

More on Charles Babbage

Charles Babbage was a mathematician famed for his inventions and ideas. His Difference Engine No.1 was the first successful automatic calculator ever made and he is sometimes referred to as the ‘father of computing’, thus being recognized by his brilliant idea of turning binary information into instructions.

More on Ada Lovelace

Lovelace was born 1815 the daughter of well-known poet, Lord Byron. Her thorough understanding in mathematics had granted her the chance of meeting Mary Somerville, an astonishing mathematician who was able to translate Laplace’s work into English, and whose texts were used in Cambridge. As a result to their encounter, Lovelace was able to discover Babbage’s proposal for the Analytical Engine, which at the time only a few people were interested in the universality of Babbage’s ideas.

1890

Herman Hollerith of the US census adapted both Jacquard and Babbage’s technologies, and with the newly discovered usage of electricity, he was able to make cards consisting of each person’s census data. The astonishing process took him approximately eight years to manually record every U.S citizen’s data. He then later turned his invention into a business, which is now known as IBM.

More on Herman Hollerith

Herman Hollerith was born in 1860 and died in 1929. He was a US census who worked as a statistician for the Census Office in his twenties. During his early work experiences, he and his mentor John Shaw Billings both agreed that the Census Office needed an easier way to collect and store data. Impressed by Jacquard’s automatic looming machine, he created his own invention: the ‘electric tabulating machine’, which was able to provide more statics in a lower amount of time. After foreign countries such as Canada, Norway, and Austria were desperate to seize Hollerith’s technology, Hollerith formed the ‘Tabulating Machine Company’, knowing his revolutionary invention should be kept to a minimum of usage. His company was later renamed as ‘International Business Machines Corporation’, also known as IBM.

1923

At the end of WWI, a German engineer named Arthur Scherbius created the Enigma machine. The Enigma machine was able to encode and decode messages and was used as a message transmitter by Nazi Germany in WWII. It was later declared commercially available, which was a big mistake by the Germans as it made it easier for British mathematician Alan Turing to decode German messages, which was said to shorten the war by approximately two years. This was the first time coding has been used as a weapon-like feature for war.

1936

Scherbius’ Enigma machine was designed for only one task; its purpose was to send hidden messages which could be converted by ally military forces. After Alan Turing cracked the code, he planned to construct his own more flexible version of the machine which would follows multiple instructions on a long piece of memory tape.

More on Alan Turing

Born in 2 Warrington Crescent, London, Alan Turing has dedicated his entire life on computer science and cognitive science. He graduated Sherborne School and studied mathematics in King’s College, Cambridge. He invented abstract computing machines, now renamed Turing’s Machines, when he was 22. He strongly believed that his invention was capable of solving any correct mathematical method, and stands by his statement when mathematician David Hilbert challenged his claim. Turing and Austrian logician Kurt Godel later crushed Hilbert after they proved that Hilbert’s ideal was impossible to satisfy. As Germany grew hostile and threatened Great Britain, Alan was recruited to the headquarters of the Government Code and Cypher School (GC&CS) at Bletchley Park, Buckinghamshire, where he did good by decoding enemy messages. Shortly after, he was invited to work in the National Physical Laboratory, where he built more computers and studied artificial intelligence and more. At an age of 42, he died in 1954 due to Cyanide poisoning.

1948

Turing wasn’t able to complete his project due to the fact that it is impossible of building a memory of instructions out of paper tape. However, engineers at Manchester University worked out a way to store memory with the use of electric charges. Combined with Turing’s ideas, they built the first multi-purpose computer. The computer was named ‘Baby’ and contained 128 bytes of memory.

1951

Improvements were made on ‘Baby’ and it was soon sold commercially as Ferranti Mark 1. BBC had captured the earliest known recording of digital music played by the computer, the few songs it played included God Save the King, Baa Baa Black Sheep, and In the Mood. British computer scientist Christopher Strachey, a friend of Alan Turing, had coded the music program. This was the first case which coding had been used as entertainment.

1961

Three programmers from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology were the creators of the well-known video game Spacewar. They designed and experimented the video game with the use of PDP-1--a fairly small computer at the time but still the size of two refrigerators. Spacewar was based on two players who controls their own separate spaceship, and their goal is to destroy the other person’s spaceship without dying first. The game’s concept promoted many other games that exists now such as first-person shooter games and real-time action.

1966

Smaller and more efficient computers were made to specialize in certain instructions. For example, The Apollo Guidance Computer System was designed for the Nasa Apollo space programme. The computer was considered usable in 1966, and within three years of complex testing and improving it took a major role in helping Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin reach the surface of the Moon. It had only 74KB of memory and was able to control a 13,000kg spaceship, orbiting at 3,500km/h around the Moon, and landing it safely back to Earth.

1971

The first commercial microprocessor was released by Intel. Intel was able to pack the memory and processing power of computers into tiny chips. The chips were originally made for a Japanese electronic calculator, however it wasn’t long till programmers start to realize that these chips can be used in a wide spread of machines--including personal computers.

1981

The popularity of computing and coding was on the rise. BBC released BBC Micro, and more than 1.5 million units were sold to the public. Its sole purpose was to bring computing and coding to homes and schools around the country.

1984

Architects began using computer aided design (CAD) programs to help design and draft bold new structures such as the Schlumberger Research Centre in Cambridge. Instead of using paper drawings and handmade models, computers allowed architects to design and test different material and construction techniques more accurately without being at the expense of making mistakes.

1990

Computers were able to manipulate and store large amount of data, which had made it easier for scientists to explore and experiment new various ideas and claims. For example, the Human Genome Project which although had lasted over a decade, allowed the DNA sequence of each fragment to be researched and pieced together by scientists at the University of California Santa Cruz.

1991

British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee invented the ‘world wide web’, a system for linking documents and information via hyperlinks. It could run on almost any computer and since Berners-Lee did not seek for a patent, his creation went viral and over 100,000 websites worldwide were created within five years. Today there are estimated to be in excess of half a billion.

1997

As the number of web pages expanded largely, more reliable and accurate information became harder to find. That’s why Larry Page and Sergey Brin, two students at Stanford University, invented a system which can measure the popularity of web pages. Page and Brin named their asset ‘Google’, and as its popularity increased dramatically, they turned it into a business venture. It is now the most favored search engine in the entire world today.

2008

Smartphones can now store 100 million times as much information as original electronic computers. They’re also more efficient in use, and consists of applications with brand new features such as temperature, stopwatch and much more. Apple launched the first version of the App Store in July 2008, its most desired apps includes Facebook, with 10 million downloads in its first weekend.
